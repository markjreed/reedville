UR-AMBONISCHES WÖRTERVERZEICHNIS

An index to reconstructions in Stresemann's (1927) Die Lauterscheinungen in den Ambonischen Sprachen (Zeitschift für Eingeborenen Sprachen, Beihefte 10)

 

I compiled this index to Stresemann's Lauterscheinungen back in the 1970s, when, working on Lettinese and other eastern Indonesian languages, I needed a quick finder-list of his reconstructions. I've been pleased to see, over the years, that it has also proven useful to other investigators. Still, while it may already be known to researchers in the area from fading Xerox copies, I have decided to make it available in this more permanent and more legible form.

Admittedly by this point, perhaps, it may have only archival value, since to some extent, like Stresemann's work itself, it has been superseded by the great amount of research carried out in the Ambonese and eastern Indonesian areas in recent years. I would mention in particular James Collins' Chicago dissertation (since published, 1983, as Pacific Linguistics D-47). And happily, too, thanks in large part to the dedicated workers of the SIL, it is no longer true-- as I wrote in 1977-- that "the Ambonese area is one of the least-investigated of AN-speaking areas;" nor must we deal anymore with "the amateurish nature of some of [Stresemann's] 19th Century sources," aside, of course, from their abiding historical value.

Stresemann's classification of Ur-Ambon (UA) into subgroups "Sub-Ambon, Sub-Seran, Sub-Buru", is probably no longer valid, but since I am following his format, those terms are used here (abbreviated SA, SS, SB in the list). Others have noted, I believe, what I pointed out in his "Sub-Buru" as

an apparent methodological error...: AN *ǵ [Dempwolff's symbol, modern *j], is said to have merged with *r and *l > UA *l. But in the three Sub-Buru languages, the *l < *ǵ goes to 0, while the *l < *r,l remains unchanged, [implying] either (1) the SB languages should have been excluded from the Ambonese family...or (2) a separate UA reflex of *ǵ should have been reconstructed.

The list also contains forms which appear to be contradictory in Stremann's own terms-- viz. doublets with *r ~ *l or *d ~ *l; perhaps these result from undetected loanwords in the daughter languages, if they are not simply omissions of the subgroup label-- e.g. without "SA," a form is assumed to be "UA". For the sake of consistency, I follow Stresemann's orthography (most nowadays would prefer "mb, nd, ŋg" for his "b, d, g"), but for typographical convenience, I substitute "ŋ" for his "n+superscript dot" = velar nasal, and "q" for his apostrophe = final glottal stop (and note that from Collins' and others' work, it has become clear that more final consonantss were retained in this area than Stresemann allowed for).

Note that no account is taken of Dempwolff's *ṭ or *ṇṭ (Dyen's *T, nT, now deprecated); "*h" reflects Dempwolff's set of correspondences, not necessarily those of modern *q; and modern *h or *S (Dempwolff's spiritus lenis) is completely absent.

We give a brief English gloss, the original German gloss, and the page number (a few are missing, sorry) where the item first appears. (One or two glosses are educated guesses, either on my part, or by some German friends-- even they found some of Stresemann's usages puzzling.) I have not deemed it necessary to include AN etyma. It might have been desirable to include Collins' reconstructions, but that's a job for another time or another person.
