Ecë nin?

What you have written here is mpe nni, which I find confusing.

First, in Quenya, the vowels go over the preceding letter, not the following one. That means you need a short carrier for words that start with a vowel, and then you need to shift your vowels one consonant to the left.

Second, c (which is always pronounced k in Quenya) is represented by the tengwa a. You have what looks like r, which represents mp. Or, if the tall stem is not intentional, then you have t, which represents just m (without the p).

Finally, the second vowel in ecë is the same as the first. It is written with the diaeresis purely for the benefit of readers accustomed to English spelling conventions, to reinforce the fact that it's not silent.

So ecë nin is pronounced "eckeh nin", and the Tengwar is this:

`FaF 5G5

Hanta

The entry for hantalë says "A verbal stem #hanta- "thank, give thanks" may apparently be isolated from this word." The # is just a notational convention in the dictionary indicating that the following form is not attested directly, but reverse-engineered from some other form. It's like assuming the English word "give" exists when all you've ever seen is "giving". But the # is not part of the word.

So, from hantalë we deduce that there's a verb hanta for "thank". In order to actually use that verb we must conjugate it.

First, the tense. In English, when we extend "thanks" or "thank you" to a full sentence, We normally say "I thank you". But that's a special case; for normal verbs, "I do it" is a general statement, and you say "I am doing it" in order to indicate that you're doing it right now. So we will use the Elvish equivalent of "I am thanking you (right now)" as opposed to "I thank you (as a routine occurrance)". To get that, we stick an -ë- before the final -a: hantëa. But that could mean "am thanking", "is thanking", "are thanking", etc; we need to indicate who is doing the thanking. In this case, that's "I", which is indicated by adding -n. So we have hantëan for "I am thanking". But who is being thanked? "You", presumably, but now we have some options...

In Elvish, as in many European languages, choice of second person form depends on singular/plural and familiar/polite. One difference is that unlike the European norm, you don't have to know someone in order to use the familiar; you just have to consider yourself superior to them, which is not uncommon for Elves. A random Dwarf or Hobbit would likely get the familiar treatment.

Meldahantalë

Not sure what "Thanksgiving to my friend" is meant to convey here. Could you clarify?

Also, the word melda appears to be an adjective meaning "beloved", "dear", "sweet", so melda hantalë would be "beloved thanksgiving". The noun "friend" has many translations, but the one corresponding to to melda is meldo (masculine) or meldë (feminine). Do be careful with connotation, however, as it can also mean "lover". (In this I guess it is similar to the Galactic Hitch-hiker slang verb "sass", which means "know", "be aware of", "meet", or "have sex with".)

Mára

To write mára in Tengwar, start with an m (t). The á is long, so next you write a long carrier (~). Then you put the a on top of the long carrier, then write a r (7), and then put another a on top of the r. End result:

t~C7C
